## Writing 

### Book Review 

#### Don’t Make Me Think 

Steve Krug published the revised version of “Don’t Make Me Think” in 2013 as a renewal of the 2006 version, for some previously intuitive examples no longer existed, and several revolutionary technologies became popular, especially as the edgy smartphones gradually became mainstream along with the countless mobile applications, changing various aspects of people’s lives. The rapid change in the tech world motivated Krug to compose a third version of the book, implementing new ideas and principles while maintaining the goal of inspiring designers to build more usable products and make the interactions more straightforward for the users. 

After I read the preface, I was immediately attracted, not only because the nostalgic pictures reminded me of the Internet services I had as a child but also because I deeply agreed with Krug's messages: respect the user’s habits, simplify the flow, and provide clear and concise assistance. It felt familiar to me as the core belief coincided with NNGroup’s Usability Heuristics for UI Design, which I learned a few weeks ago in my UI 2 class. 

I was profoundly inspired by the portion “Make it obvious what’s clickable” in Chapter 3\. The author’s words made me realize a fatal mistake in my final project for the previously mentioned class, “In general, you’ll be fine if you… make sure that their shape and location identify them as clickable.” Krug believes that people tend to parse everything they see for interactive clues. Clearly distinguishing clickable components from others is a critical aspect of usability and, hence, a major task for the designers. 

The “Wild West” part of the timeline from 1997 to 2002 notified me of my lack of consideration. I forgot to unify the style of my buttons – each clickable component had a drastically different appearance. Mimicking the style of clickable text in the examples of “The Golden Age,” I reduced the types of buttons in my prototype to four and dedicated one typeface for all clickable components. The web page looked significantly neater after I followed this important rule.   
Another portion that affected my mindset was Krug’s explanation about navigation, where I found a second flaw in my prototype: not all pages straightforwardly informed the user where they were. I soon fixed the issue and made improvements with other suggestions Krug offered in this chapter. For instance, the site logo is now interactive and can bring the user directly back to the homepage, which is intuitive but mistakenly ignored previously. 

In conclusion, I greatly enjoyed this book and believe it is well-written. Krug’s writing style is easy to comprehend, just like the principle of conciseness he mentioned in Chapter 5\. It never felt to me that I was reading sophisticated professional instructions or being struck with obscure terms. Instead, it felt like I was talking with the author without the awkwardness of stuttering. The frequent use of images also improved my reading experience to some extent. They provided buffer zones so that I was never overwhelmed with overloaded textual content. Briefly said, this was one of my best reading experiences recently. 

### Film Analysis 

#### Under the Sun 

I watched the film Under the Sun, directed by Vitaly Mansky. It was a documentary produced by several studios and agencies, including Saxonia Entertainment. The current version, released at the end of October 2015, can be viewed for free on tubitv.com. The word “complicated” best summarizes my overall impression of the film’s use of color. As North Korea ordered the original work as a propaganda tool, some parts of the film used vibrant and saturated warm colors with an emphasis on various shades of red. The rest of the film is about behind-the-scenes stories of the filmmaking process that reveal the factual life in Pyongyang. For those portions, Mansky generally used more dull, low-saturated, cool colors, explicitly using a massive amount of white and desaturated cyan and teal.   
   
The scene that caught my attention the most was when the protagonist’s family had a scripted meal together. The director used many ingenuities to make it a heart-warming parent-children activity scene. However, the logical thinking and details of the shot made it one of the most ironic scenes of the entire film, as an ordinary family of three had an exaggerated nine-course lunch with huge bowls of soup. 

There was this close-up shot of their food, including strong contrasts between the unsaturated white dishes, less-saturated blue table, highly saturated red kimchi soup, and extremely saturated brown rice cakes. The wooden floor in the background reflected a low-saturated tint of yellow-orange. As expected, almost all elements in the frame were warmer in color. The only components that involved transparency were the three glasses. They could easily be perceived as transparent objects because the parts of the table underneath the glasses had less brightness but the same hue and saturation. 

The composition of the dishes was also worth mentioning. The dishes with red and orange colors were put at the center of the scene so that they could straightforwardly attract the audience’s attention. The second thing that the viewer would likely pay attention to was the plate of fried eggs at the bottom-left corner of the frame. Its bright, mildly saturated yellow color was much more active than most of the other food on the table. Overall, this scene is ordinarily composed as it was not set up to convey the ideas of aesthetics.   
    
As I mentioned at the beginning of this analysis, the film had two different color tones. They could be represented by the two scenes above. A larger portion of Under the Sun, which focuses on North Korea’s social reality, uses an opposite coloring style from the previously mentioned scene. In the propaganda part, the tone was either beige, which created a relaxing atmosphere for the narrative, or a shade of red, as the people’s clothes at the ceremonies commonly used saturated red, bright brown, and black. As for the rest, the tones were mostly low-saturated cyan, teal, or gray. It could render a more depressing mood as those colors are not only passive but low in chroma, too. 

Analyzing a film is different from critiquing a two-dimensional work of art, primarily because a film is made up of a great number of 2D works. Therefore, analyzing a film is equivalent to comprehending a series of 2D works that build upon each other. Each frame plays a different role in telling the story – it cannot be truly analyzed without the context of the film’s plot. After watching the entire film, the biggest surprise was that I realized two entirely different stories could also share a single timeline. The miserable reality could, in fact, increase the level of irony of scripted propaganda.
